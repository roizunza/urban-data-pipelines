# Theoretical Framework and Analytical Foundations

This project employs the *Urban Inference Engine*, which is defined as an algorithmic engine designed to estimate urban parameters such as building scale and height, from spatial variables and underlying infrastructure. Its operation is not random; it is based on consolidated theories from various fields of urban theory, such as spatial econometrics and quantitative geography. Likewise, it integrates methodologies from complex analytical disciplines—such as remote sensing and parametric statistics—to approach and decode the urban fabric.

Below are the theories that nourish the algorithm's operation:

**1. Spatial Economics and Induced Density (Bid-Rent Theory)**
The algorithm utilizes the **Bid-Rent Theory** postulated by William Alonso (1964). This theory states that land price and building density are inversely proportional to the distance from economic activity centers. In the model, the concentration of financial and tourist Points of Interest (POIs) acts as a mathematical proxy for the "Central Business District". Parcels (polygons) with a higher density of POIs within a walkable radius absorb enormous real estate pressure, forcing the algorithm to extrude the volume vertically to maximize the plot's profitability.

**2. Space Syntax and Road Carrying Capacity**
To model the restriction of built volume based on streets, the model incorporates foundations of **Space Syntax Theory** (Hillier and Hanson, 1984). This theory establishes that the road network is the morphological skeleton that dictates the distribution of land uses and density. Roads with a higher degree of topological integration (primary and secondary avenues) possess a greater "carrying capacity" to absorb vehicular and pedestrian flows, conditioning the viability of supporting massive vertical densities. Conversely, lower-order roads (residential streets or paths) impose physical restrictions. Within the algorithmic engine, the topologically extracted road hierarchy operates as a restrictive or enhancing coefficient of the allowed scale according to its classification.

**3. Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) and Spatial Friction**
To model the influence of mass transit infrastructure (monorail and subway lines), the model is based on the principles of Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) (Calthorpe, 1993), postulating that high-density urbanization should orbit within walkable distances from stations.
In traditional urban planning, "Euclidean circular buffers" are calculated—theoretical radii drawn in a straight, flat line from the station. Given Chongqing's characteristic context and topography, applying linear radii results in an uncertain interpretation. Therefore, the algorithm applies the concept of Spatial Friction (derived from Waldo Tobler's First Law of Geography), which states that movement across the territory requires a cost in effort or energy. Using Digital Elevation Models (DEM - SRTM) and spatial indexing algorithms (K-D Trees), the model calculates a base distance with a detour coefficient that simulates the path of the pedestrian network, and applies a formula that penalizes the movement cost by assigning an equivalent of 10 horizontal meters for every vertical meter ascended ($\Delta Z \times 10$), thus modeling the stations' actual topographic catchment area.

**4. Spatial Multicriteria Evaluation (Spatial MCDA)**
To integrate the aforementioned variables (commercial density, distance to nodes, and street capacity), a Spatial Multicriteria Evaluation (Spatial MCDA) was employed, standardized by J. Malczewski (1999). The model takes the variables that bound the territorial environment, normalizing them and assigning them a value to then weight them under a common scale (0-100 points) through a Weighted Linear Combination (WLC).
In the inference engine's code, this equation translates into a 40% weight for transit accessibility (with its respective topographic friction), 30% for POI density, and 30% for road hierarchy. This allows decision-making on a polygon-by-polygon basis: a building may have high commercial demand, but if the road hierarchy vector (low-capacity road) or the terrain elevation act as barriers that drastically increase the accessibility effort, the MCDA penalizes the final score, restricting the inference of a skyscraper where the physical infrastructure does not support it.

**5. Satellite Validation (Ground-Truthing)**
The geometric validation of the inferences is performed by cross-referencing the results with satellite spectrometry data, using a luminescence index: 
* **VIIRS (Nighttime Lights):** Using the Day/Night Band (DNB) of the VIIRS instrument aboard the Suomi NPP satellite, the radiance of light emissions in wavelengths from 0.5 to 0.9 micrometers is captured. The DNB sensor is highly sensitive to continuous anthropogenic lighting. Spatial academic literature (Elvidge et al., 2012) justifies the use of this metric as a direct proxy for local Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and built density, proving that there is a linear mathematical correlation between the intensity of photons detected by the satellite sensor and the volume of socioeconomic activity and energy consumption concentrated on the underlying terrestrial surface.
